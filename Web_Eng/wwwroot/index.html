Purpose of this webpage

Our Team has developed a small webpage that will provide travelers to learn more about one of the historical cities of Germany

Münster

Ulm Minster is a famous Gothic cathedral located in the city of Ulm, Germany. It is the tallest church in the world, with a height of 161.5 meters (530 feet), and is one of the most impressive examples of Gothic architecture in Germany.

Albert Einstein

Albert Einstein was a German-born theoretical physicist who is widely regarded as one of the most influential scientists of the 20th century. He developed the theory of general relativity and made groundbreaking contributions to our understanding of space, time, and the nature of the universe. Einstein was born in Ulm

University City

The University of Ulm was founded in 1967 and has since grown to become a world-class research institution. It is particularly well-known for its programs in medicine, natural sciences, and engineering. The university is consistently ranked as one of the top universities in Germany and Europe, and its graduates are highly sought-after by employers.

Danube River

Ulm is located on the Danube River (in German, Donau), which is the second-longest river in Europe after the Volga. The Danube flows through ten countries, including Germany, Austria, Hungary, and Romania, before emptying into the Black Sea.

History



Beginning of Ulm

“Actum Hulmam palatio regio in Dei nomine feliciter. Amen” This Latin phrase is the start of Ulm’s “official” history: On July 22nd, 854, King Ludwig the German seals a document in the palatinate Ulma – the first known mention of today’s metropolis. As yet the small settlement around the imperial palace bears no resemblance to the future city. A few huts and farms grace the banks of Danube and Blau rivers

Visionary or megalomaniac? Ulm is building a minster!

On June 30th, 1377, Ulm citizens act upon this realization: the foundation for the Ulm Münster cathedral is laid. The construction demonstrates an immense confidence: the rising city, home to around 10.000 residents, builds a gigantic cathedral holding up to 20.000 people. The entire project is financed by citizens’ donations.

Industrialisation and federal fortress: Ulm is gaining relevance again

By the first half of the 19th century hardly anything remains of the former splendor and significance: the powerful imperial city has deteriorated into a provincial town with only 12.000 residents. But quite soon Ulm regains the historical limelight: between 1842 and1859 a workforce of close to 8000 builds the mighty federal fortress with 41 fortifications and a wall with a total length of 9 kilometers. The huge fortress construction site as well as the railroad connection in 1850 lead to great economic development. The second half of the 19th century in Ulm marks the establishment of companies like Magirus, Wieland and Kässbohrer, known around the world.

Sightseeing


The Blautopf


A glance is enough: there is something magical about this place. Blue and green flow incomparably into each other. Like a shining eye from the depths of the earth, the blue pot is a source of myths and fairy tales. One of the most beautiful is probably the “History of the Beautiful Lau”, in which Eduard Mörike tells how a water fox at the blue pot learns to laugh again. A stone sculpture on the shore reminds of her . . . and even today it is said to be seen briefly in the depths of the spring funnel on some days.

The Fischer- and Gerberviertel


Nowhere else can you forget the present as nicely as on a walk through the fishing district of Ulm. A few steps south of the modern city center begins its enchanted world, in which half-timbered houses are surrounded by water. Glittering blue, dripping mill wheels, sparrow chirping. The alleys run up to the gates of the city wall, where the magnificent view of the Danube opens. All this makes the former craftsmen's quarter the most popular attraction after the Münster.
